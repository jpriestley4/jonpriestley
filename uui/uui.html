Project Overview

The goal of this project was to gain a detailed understanding of user behaviour when organising an international road trip to inform a set of design guidelines for future travel planning applications.

The theoretical approaches of External Cognition (EC) and Information Foraging Theory (IFT) were applied to frame the data collection and analysis and inform a set of design guidelines to improve future digital technologies within the travel space.

Frameworks

External Cognition (EC)

  • EC posits that cognitive processes often involve a ‘back and forth’ interactive relationship between the mind, body and environment.
  • This framework looks at how human cognition can be externalised by using either physical tools, such as a pencil and paper, or digital tools, such as mapping, calendar or note taking applications.
  • Observing users organise an international roadtrip from an EC perspective is suitable due to the broad and complicated nature of the task providing ample opportunity for the use of ERs to offload cognition.

Information Foraging Theory (IFT)

  • IFT explains information search and exploration behaviour. The framework is an adaption of the behavioural ecology model, ‘Optimal Foraging Theory’ (OFT) which predicts how an animal behaves when searching for food.
  • Using this analogy, users are considered as ‘informavores’ that ‘forage’ for information just as animals forage for food.
  • The central premise of IFT is that users make trade-off decisions to maximise the value of information gained from information sources while minimising the cost of cognitive effort and time expended.

Study Design

Participants

Three participants were recruited for this study, each with varying degrees of experience in organising road trips and using journey planning technology.

Observation Setting

Observations were conducted remotely over Zoom and participants were asked if they were happy to have their interactions recorded.

All observations were conducted in natural environments. Participants were asked to complete the tasks as they would do normally, using any digital or physical tools available to them. Since EC and IFT require different observation approaches, separate observations were conducted for each framework.
