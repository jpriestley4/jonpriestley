Context

"Investing without research is like playing stud poker and never looking at the cards" - Peter Lynch

In the finance industry, retail investors have been typically labelled as ‘dumb money’ because they tend to base their investment decisions on instinct rather than research. This is largely due to retail investors not having enough time, experience, or patience to conduct investment research. Unfortunately, such behaviour negatively affects investment returns.

Renowned money manager, Peter Lynch, argues that retail investors are able to pick winning stocks just as well as Wall Street experts by taking notice of their immediate world around them and doing just a little research into a company's financial statements.

For many amateur retail investors, financial statement analysis has been made out to be esoteric and technical. However, once equipped with the understanding of what different key numbers mean, reviewing financial statements becomes part of a broader and more intuitive process of interpreting a story surrounding a company.

Today, many decision support systems do little to empower amateur investors with interpreting financial information. Instead, these systems are typically designed to offload the user's cognitive processing, resulting in slow and ineffective learning.

Project Aim

  1. Research the interaction behaviour and rationale of expert financial analysts when interpreting financial statements.
  2. Address this behaviour in a prototype designed to support amateur investors.

Objectives

To achieve this aim, I established the following objectives:

  1. To understand the interaction behaviour and rationale of expert financial analysts when interpreting financial statements.
  2. Establish design guidelines in line with the research findings.
  3. Produce a high-fidelity prototype that supports amateur investors in interpreting financial statements.
  4. Evaluate the usefulness of the prototype by observing amateur investors using the prototype to interpret financial statements.

Research

Desk Research

I began the research process by conducting some desk research to identify appropriate methods that I can use to understand the behaviour and rationale of expert analysts when performing financial statement analysis. It was important to explore methods that emphasised a naturalistic approach whereby participants are observed in their natural environments. One method that stood out was contextual inquiry.

Contextual Inquiry

I employed a contextual inquiry approach by interviewing a sample of eight expert financial analysts as they performed financial statement analysis in their natural environment. This involved participants analysing the financial statements of a company that they needed to analyse now or in the near future and then deciding on whether or not to invest in that company.

The interviews were conducted remotely via zoom.

Four principles

This study collectively applied the following four principles help to deliver rich qualitative insights that would later inform design decisions.

  • Context: Capture an ongoing experience rather than summary experience and concrete data as opposed to abstract data.
  • Partnership: Collaborate with the participant to develop a shared understanding of the participant’s work.
  • Interpretation: Assign meaning to insights gathered by infering possible system designs which can support the participant’s work structure.
  • Focus: Have a clear interview framework to steer the conversation towards data that is relevant for the study.

Thematic Analysis

I used a thematic analysis approach to assess I systematically analysed the behaviour and rationale of the expert investors using the following thematic analysis process.

Thematic Analysis Process

  1. Transcribed, read, re-read and wrote down initial ideas.
  2. Used Dovetail to systematically generate initial codes relating to interesting features of the data and collated relevant data into each code.
  3. Collated codes into potential themes
  4. Reviewed the themes by looking at the underlying data under each theme and checking that the themes made sense in relation to the coded extracts.
  5. Defined and named the themes to help tell the overall story of the analysis.
  6. Produced the analysis report by selecting compelling and vivid extract examples.

Dovetail: Canvas View

Themes

From the data collected, I picked out the following six themes which reflect the behaviour and rationale of expert investors performing financial statement analysis.

  1. Framing and reframing an account or story of a company.
  2. Developing and applying an investment strategy to guide a focused process.
  3. Acquiring contextual information surrounding a company, its industry, its related countries and the stock market that it trades in.
  4. Applying intuition to help make sense of uncertain information.
  5. Applying analytical techniques to assess a company's past performance and current financial position.
  6. Referencing a variety of resources

Design Guidelines

Based on the findings from the data analysis, I created the following design guidelines to inform the design of the prototype.

  1. Support users in framing and reframing of a company’s financial information.
  2. Support users in developing and applying an investment strategy.
  3. Support users in acquiring a company’s contextual information.
  4. Support users in developing and applying their intuitive tacit knowledge.
  5. Support users in applying a variety of analytical techniques.
  6. Support users in referencing a variety of information resources.

Design

Conceptual Design

During the conceptual design stage, my focus was on ‘getting the right design’ as opposed to ‘getting the design right’. Therefore, I started the design process by sketching low-fidelity conceptual wireframes onto a whiteboard. This quick and dirty approach helped me to think more openly and creatively about how the design guidelines can be implemented into the prototype.

Design Library

With the sketches as a guide, I designed a high-fidelity clickable prototype using Figma. To showcase the full range of features and functionality that would be available in a final product, I designed the prototype to a highly detailed level. To ensure consistency in the visual design of the prototype, I created a design library in Figma which incorporated guides for colour, typography and grids along with a range of reusable functional components.

Using Disney as a use case.

I used Disney as a use case for illustrating how the product can support amateur investors in interpreting a company’s financial statements. I chose Disney for the following reasons:

  • Many amateur investors will have some understanding of Disney as a business due to its significant presence in popular culture, allowing me to see how effective the prototype is supporting the user's pre-existing knowledge.
  • Disney has a broad and complicated business which gives amateur investors plenty of room to use the breadth of the features on the prototype to develop more nuanced and refined story of the company.

Using an introductory video

I also decided to integrate a short video into the prototype to bring awareness to the breadth of features available on the prototype. This helped to support a more naturalistic evaluation.

Evaluation

Goal

The goal of the evaluation was to measure the usefulness of the prototype in supporting amateur investors with interpreting financial statements to make an informed investment decision.

Data Collection Method

  • Remote moderated user tests, including:
  • Pre- and post-test likert scale questionnaire designed to measure change in confidence levels.
  • Naturalistic observations with think aloud approach.

Analysis Method

  • Group average change: Measuring the change in the mean average value for the pre-test and post-test responses across the entire sample of participants.

  • Individual changes: I also compared the change in confidence levels of each participant to see the variance amongst the sample.

Questionnaire Design

The questions that I used in the pre- and post-test questionnaire were modified from another questionnaire which measured confidence towards learning mathematics. As such, participants were asked to rate their level of agreement to eleven statements surrounding their confidence towards financial statement analysis.

Findings

Across the entire sample of participants, there was an increase in average confidence levels in interpreting financial statements after using the prototype from a pre-test level of 3.01 (neutral confidence) to a post-test confidence level of 3.90 (high confidence).

On a more granular level, all participants experienced an increase in confidence levels in interpreting financial statements after using the prototype.

Summary Notes

  • Expert analysts were found to conduct financial statement analysis in an intuitive manner of framing and reframing a company’s information to make an informed investment decision.
  • Based on these findings and the findings of related literature, I established a set of six design guidelines to inform the design of a prototype.
  • By showing a statistically significant increase in the confidence levels of amateur investors after using the prototype, the evaluation revealed that these guidelines are useful in supporting amateur investors with interpreting financial statements.
